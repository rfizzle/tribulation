# Tribulation — Vision

> *"Survive what comes next."* — Minecraft's difficulty, remade as a curve that
> climbs the whole life of a world.

## The promise

Vanilla Minecraft is at its hardest the first night and only gets easier. Tribulation
turns that upside down: the world pushes back harder the longer you live, the farther you
roam from spawn, and the deeper you dig. Every hostile mob is sized up the instant it
spawns, from your own hard-won progress — so an hour-one zombie and a week-two zombie are
not the same fight. On top of raw numbers sit unlockable mob powers, dread-filled Blood
Moon nights, and death penalties you can leave off or turn all the way up. If you want
survival that never stops asking more of you, this is for you. If you want a world that
eventually goes quiet, it is not.

## The arc of a world

The first hours feel like vanilla, because they are — you dig in, make a bed, outlast the
first nights. What you notice, slowly, is that the mobs near you keep pace with you. By the
mid game the creatures around your base have grown teeth you did not sign up for, a full-moon
night carries real risk, and venturing a thousand blocks out or sinking to bedrock is a step
up in threat, not just a change of scenery. Late game is mastery under pressure: a maxed-out
world where zombies sprint you down, skeletons loose flaming arrows, packs coordinate, and
named champion mobs stalk the dark. You are never safe by default again — only prepared, or
not.

## The features, as you'll play them

### The world that grows deadlier

- **What it is** — every hostile mob's health, damage, armor, and speed are set at spawn
  from four things about you and where you are: how long you have survived (your level,
  climbing from 0 to 250 at about one hour of play per level), how far you stand from world
  spawn (past 1,000 blocks, up to +150% threat), how far from sea level you have climbed or
  dug (up to +50%), and the moon overhead on Overworld nights (up to +10%, peaking at full
  moon). The Nether, the End, the deep dark, and dangerous structures add their own menace
  on top.
- **How you use it** — you just play. Survive longer, wander farther, delve deeper, and the
  world answers in kind. Nothing to craft or toggle.
- **What changes for you** — distance and depth stop being free. A far-flung base or a
  bedrock mine is a standing danger, and the harder the fight, the richer the reward. A rare
  few scaled mobs spawn as named **champions** with special powers and triple experience.

### Mob tiers and the powers they unlock

- **What it is** — five tiers gate at levels 50, 100, 150, 200, and 250. As your level
  crosses each line, the mobs around you gain new abilities: zombies call reinforcements
  and later break down wooden doors, skeletons switch to swords and later loose flaming
  arrows, spiders place webs and leap, creepers shorten their fuse and eventually spawn
  charged. High-tier mobs of the same kind also hunt in packs — hurt one and it alerts its
  neighbors, and natural groups spawn larger.
- **How you use it** — you cross tiers by surviving. When a new power comes online, a brief
  in-game heads-up names exactly what changed, so you are never blindsided.
- **What changes for you** — the same monsters demand new tactics at every tier. Optional
  **Environmental Pressure** (off unless you switch it on) sharpens this at higher tiers:
  night mobs gain keener senses and their hits can sap your strength or slow you.

### Blood Moon nights

- **What it is** — on a full-moon nightfall there is a chance (one in four by default) the
  night turns into a Blood Moon. The sky and moon bleed red, twice as many hostiles pour in,
  the moon's threat bonus triples, and you cannot sleep until dawn.
- **How you use it** — you survive it, or you hole up. A warning announces the Blood Moon as
  it rises; there is no skipping the night through a bed. Server operators can force one on
  or off on demand.
- **What changes for you** — a handful of nights per world become genuine sieges you plan
  around, not another night to sleep through.

### When you die

- **What it is** — a suite of death consequences, most of them optional. **Death Relief** is
  on by default and merciful: each death eases your difficulty down a couple of levels, so a
  rough patch bends the curve back toward you. The punishing layers are all opt-in — a slow
  **level decay** while you are away for days, **hardcore hearts** that strip permanent max
  health each death, and a **soul inventory** that destroys everything you carried unless it
  is protected.
- **How you use it** — you choose your stakes before the world begins, or a server sets them
  for everyone. **Heart Fragments** restore health lost to hardcore hearts; the **Soulbound**
  enchantment marks the items a soul inventory lets you keep.
- **What changes for you** — death means whatever you decide it means, from a gentle
  rubber-band all the way to losing your gear and your hearts for good.

### Turning the dial: Shatter and Ascendant Shards

- **What it is** — two items that put the difficulty curve in your hands. A **Shatter Shard**
  is a rare drop (about a 0.5% chance from higher-level mobs) that lowers your difficulty
  when used; an **Ascendant Shard**, crafted from a Nether Star ringed by four Shatter
  Shards, does the opposite, jumping your difficulty up.
- **How you use it** — right-click a Shatter Shard to step down five levels when the world
  has outrun you; right-click an Ascendant Shard to leap twenty-five levels when you want the
  fight — and the rewards — to escalate faster than an hour-per-level.
- **What changes for you** — mercy and ambition both become things you can hold. Ascending
  trades survivability for deadlier mobs and richer experience and loot.

### Knowing where you stand

- **What it is** — a small badge in the corner of your screen shows your level and tier at a
  glance. Hold a key to peek a detail panel: your progress to the next tier and a live list
  of what the mobs near you can actually do at your current tier.
- **How you use it** — the badge is always there unless you turn it off. Hold **Left Alt** to
  peek the panel (rebind it under Options → Controls → Tribulation). Your first level-up and
  first tier each come with a one-time note teaching you what just happened.
- **What changes for you** — the escalating world is always legible. You can read your own
  danger and the room's danger without opening a menu or guessing.

## Staying in control

Everything is configurable, and every value takes effect the moment you change it — no
restart. The corner badge can be turned off or moved; every optional penalty ships **off**,
so a fresh world plays as pure escalation with a gentle death rebate and nothing you did not
ask for. `/tribulation info` tells you your level, tier, and progress to the next;
`/tribulation hearts` shows any health you have lost. Operators can read, set, or reset any
player's level, restore hearts, force a Blood Moon, inspect the mob they are looking at, and
reload the settings live. In multiplayer you choose how a crowd of players near one mob is
read — by the nearest, the average, or the highest. Tribulation needs no other mod to run, on
singleplayer or a dedicated server.

## Better together

Tribulation stands entirely alone; these are bonuses, never requirements, and with none of
them installed nothing is missing.

- **Prosperity** — a harder-fought world pays out better: your difficulty tier can enrich the
  loot you find, so remoteness and danger both sweeten the chest. Never required.
- **Meridian** — its keep-on-death Tether and Tribulation's Soulbound are the same promise, so
  with both installed they settle into one thing to earn instead of two. Never required.
- **Mercantile** — clerics can trade relief from the curve, selling Shatter Shards and Heart
  Fragments to players who have earned the standing. Never required.

## What Tribulation will never do

Tribulation owns one thing — how hard the world is — and stays in that lane. It will never
touch what a sibling owns: not the enchanting table (Meridian's), not villagers and trade
(Mercantile's), not the loot inside chests and containers (Prosperity's). It adds no new
dimension and demands no companion mod to function. It completes vanilla survival rather than
declaring it broken — every fight is still a vanilla fight, only scaled. And it never forces
its stakes on you: every punishing layer is opt-in, the badge turns off, and a world where you
change nothing is simply vanilla difficulty that finally keeps climbing.
